Documented Past

A Millennium of History

This summary brings together temple tradition, local historical accounts, and public references. Early dates and royal associations should be read as historical tradition unless supported by a formal archaeological publication.

Ganepurana Rajamaha Wiharaya is an ancient Buddhist temple in Bentota, on Sri Lanka's southwest coast. Local historical accounts connect the site with the Polonnaruwa period (1056–1236 AD), while the Bentota area itself is remembered as a place with a much older Buddhist cultural presence.

Temple tradition identifies Ganepurana Rajamaha Wiharaya as one of five related temples that formed a wider sacred complex on the southern bank of the Bentara River. This traditional narrative describes the complex as extending across a large area and links the temple's main phase of development with medieval royal patronage.

Public descriptions published by the Bentota Divisional Secretariat also connect the wider area with early royal and military activity. Those references are valuable for local heritage interpretation, but they should be read alongside archaeological research when presenting the story as formal history.

Local accounts state that the temple, like several neighbouring sacred sites, suffered destruction during the Portuguese period and then remained concealed in the jungle for a long time before being rediscovered in the nineteenth century.

Within the five-temple tradition, Ganepurana Rajamaha Wiharaya is identified with the main stupa (Vehera). The carved stone pillars, inscribed stones, and surviving architectural remains on the premises are among the strongest physical heritage features that support the importance of the site today.

Documented History

Key Milestones

A timeline of key events in the temple's recorded story, compiled from local historical accounts, temple tradition, and public heritage references.

Early Buddhist Activity

Bentota's history as a Buddhist centre dates to the 2nd century BC. The area has a long cultural history of religious activity on Sri Lanka's southwest coast.

2nd c. BC
1056–1236 AD

Temple Built — Polonnaruwa Period

The temple is said to have been originally built by a king in the 12th century as part of his palace. It forms the main stupa site of a five-temple complex on the Bentara River.

Five-Temple Complex

Five temples — Ganepurana, Bodhimaluwa, Wanawasa, Bodhi Sameepa, and Galapatha — form one interconnected complex of over 1,000 acres. Archaeological evidence confirms underground tunnel connections between them.

Medieval Period
16th–17th c.

Portuguese Occupation & Destruction

During the Portuguese occupation, the temple was destroyed and its valuables taken. The temple remained hidden in Bentota's jungle for around 200 years.

🔍

Rediscovery

The temple was rediscovered in the mid-19th century. The carved stone pillars at its entrance and surrounding inscribed stones were recognised as protected archaeological monuments.

Mid-19th c.
Wanawasa — 2000s

Underground Tunnel Confirmed

A garbage pit at the neighbouring Wanawasa Rajamaha Viharaya exposed a long-forgotten tunnel, providing the first physical proof of the ancient underground connections between the five temples.

Active Temple & Piriwena Today

The Ganepurana Rajamaha Wiharaya is today an active place of worship, home to a functioning Piriwena with student monks, a Dhamma School, and regular religious observances.

About the Piriwena

A Centre of Buddhist Learning

The Ganepurana Rajamaha Wiharaya is home to an active Piriwena — a traditional Buddhist monastic school where student monks pursue full-time studies in the Theravada tradition.

The curriculum covers Pali language — the language of the original Tipitaka — Abhidhamma, Buddhist history, Sinhala literature, and classical Buddhist philosophy.

Student monks range from novices beginning their monastic journey to advanced scholars preparing for national Piriwena examinations recognised by the Government of Sri Lanka.

Spiritual Offerings

Blessings & Offerings

The temple welcomes all devotees to participate in our sacred offerings. All practices are rooted in Theravada Buddhist tradition.

🌸

Flower Offerings (Mal Puja)

Offer lotus, jasmine, or frangipani blossoms before the Buddha image — a traditional expression of reverence and an acknowledgement of impermanence.

🕯️

Oil Lamp Offering (Tel Puja)

Light an oil lamp before the image house or at the Bodhi tree to symbolise the light of wisdom dispelling the darkness of ignorance.

🍚

Almsgiving (Dana)

Offer a meal to the resident monks. Contact the temple in advance to arrange a personal or community almsgiving on a Poya day or weekend.

📿

Pirith Chanting

Request the monks to chant Maha Piritha — protective suttas — for blessings of health and wellbeing for you and your family.

☸

Stupa Circumambulation

Walk clockwise around the ancient stupa in mindful meditation — a meritorious act in the Theravada tradition performed at the main Vehera.

🌳

Bodhi Tree Puja

Water and offer flowers at the Bodhi tree on the temple premises — a living connection to the Theravada tradition of venerating the Bodhi.

🧘

Meditation at the Temple

The temple grounds offer a quiet, naturally conducive environment for seated meditation, particularly in the early morning.

📖

Dhamma Listening (Bana)

Dharma sermons are delivered on Poya days and special occasions. All are welcome to listen and deepen their understanding.

Our Mission

A Temple of Heritage & Community

The Ganepurana Rajamaha Wiharaya is an ancient Buddhist temple in Bentota with a documented history reaching back to the Polonnaruwa period (1056–1236 AD). It serves as the main stupa site of a five-temple complex that once covered over 1,000 acres on the south bank of the Bentara River.

Today, the temple is an active place of worship, home to a functioning Piriwena for student monks, a Dhamma School (Daham Pasala) for local children, and a community of lay devotees from Bentota and surrounding areas.

The temple's carved stone pillars and inscribed stones are designated protected archaeological monuments under the Government of Sri Lanka.

The Complex

The Five Temples of Bentota

Five ancient temples once forming one large complex on the Bentara River — interconnected by underground tunnels, as confirmed by archaeological evidence.

⛩️

Ganepurana Rajamaha Wiharaya

Main Stupa (Vehera) site. Home to the Piriwena and protected stone monuments.

🌿

Bodhimaluwa Rajamaha Viharaya

Site where a sapling of the Sri Maha Bodhi was planted.

🏠

Wanawasa Rajamaha Viharaya

Monks' residential kutis. Site where an underground tunnel was physically confirmed.

🌳

Bodhi Sameepa Viharaya

Site of a secondary Bodhi tree.

💎

Galapatha Rajamaha Viharaya

Main image house. A relic of Maha Kassyapa is enshrined here.
